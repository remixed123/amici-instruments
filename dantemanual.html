1Welcome to Dante

Dante is the first Dualphonic Sync Synthesizer — a four-oscillator monophonic synth for iOS and macOS, with two independent hard-sync chains running in parallel.

This guide covers every panel and control. If you're new to synthesis, start at the top and read straight through. If you're looking for one specific feature, use the table of contents on the left to jump straight to it.

Dante is available three ways:

  • Standalone on iPad, iPhone and Mac — with a built-in keyboard and song player.
  • AUv3 plugin on iOS and macOS — drops into AUM, Loopy Pro, Drambo, Logic, GarageBand, Cubasis and more.

Most of what's in this guide applies to all three. Where things differ — for example, the song player only exists in standalone — that's flagged clearly in the section.

2System Requirements

  • iOS / iPadOS: 17.6 or later. Compatible with iPhone and iPad.
  • macOS: 14.6 (Sonoma) or later, Apple Silicon (M1, M2, M3, M4 …) required.
  • AUv3 host required when using Dante as a plugin (e.g. AUM, Loopy Pro, Drambo, Logic, GarageBand, Cubasis).

3Getting Started

The fastest way to make a great sound is to let Dante make one for you.

  1. Open Dante (standalone) or load it as an AUv3 plugin in your host.
  2. Tap the dice button (🎲) in the top header to open the Random Preset Generator.
  3. Pick a category (Bass, Lead, Pad, etc.) or leave it on All.
  4. Tap Generate. A fresh preset rolls into place.
  5. Play a few notes — on the built-in keyboard (standalone) or from your MIDI controller / DAW.
  6. If you like the direction but want variation, tap Tweak to nudge the current preset rather than rolling a brand new one.

When you find a sound you love, save it to your User library — see 12. Presets.

Don't worry about understanding everything yet. The dice button gives you a working sound in one tap. You can dive into per-oscillator control whenever you're ready.

4Interface Tour

A quick walkthrough of where everything lives.

Top Header

  • Preset name (left) — tap to open the Preset Browser.
  • DANTE logo (centre) — purely decorative.
  • XY Pad button — opens the expressive XY control surface.
  • ⚙️ Settings cog (immediately right of XY Pad) — opens the App Preferences dialog (see 16. App Preferences).

Toolbar

Just below the header you'll find horizontal tabs to switch between modulation panels:

  • Osc — the four oscillators (waveform, volume, fold, detune, sync mode).
  • Vol Env — volume (VCA) envelope per oscillator.
  • Filt Env — filter envelope per oscillator.
  • Filter — filter type and settings per oscillator.
  • Cut LFO — filter cutoff LFO.
  • Vol LFO — volume LFO.
  • Fold LFO — wavefold LFO.
  • Pitch LFO — pitch LFO.
  • Glide — portamento between notes.
  • Effects — reverb and delay.

Master Strip

Above the four oscillator columns you'll see:

  • Output meter — shows the current master output level (and clipping in yellow/red).
  • Gain — overall master gain.
  • SCOPE — opens the Waveform Scope (see 14. Scope).
  • 🎲 dice — opens the Random Preset Generator.
  • DUALPHONIC SYNC — cycles through valid Dualphonic Sync combinations (see 6. Dualphonic Sync).

The Four Oscillator Columns

The main area shows four oscillator columns, each in its own colour: Osc 1 Osc 2 Osc 3 Osc 4. The colour follows that oscillator everywhere in Dante — including the Scope, LFO panels, and Preset Browser.

About the white lines on parameter sliders. When an LFO is modulating a parameter, you'll see a horizontal white line move up and down inside the slider showing exactly how much the parameter is being pushed in real time. No guesswork required.

Per-Osc and Linked Modes

One concept appears everywhere in Dante, so it's worth learning straight away: most modulation panels can run in either Per-Osc or Linked mode. You toggle between the two using the button next to each panel's title — it shows the current mode (PER-OSC or LINKED).

Per-Osc mode

Each of the four oscillators gets its own independent copy of that module. So in Per-Osc Filter mode you might run a 24dB low-pass filter wide open on Osc 1, a swept notch on Osc 2, a band-pass on Osc 3 and a formant filter on Osc 4 — all at the same time. Each oscillator's controls are colour-coded so you always know which oscillator you're editing.

Linked mode

All four oscillators share a single instance of that module. One set of controls drives every oscillator at once. It's lighter on CPU, simpler to dial in, and often exactly what you want when you're after a cohesive sound rather than four contrasting voices.

Per-Osc and Linked exist for the following panels: Vol Env, Filt Env, Filter, Cut LFO, Vol LFO, Fold LFO, Pitch LFO, Glide, and Effects. The Oscillators panel itself is always per-oscillator by nature — each oscillator has its own waveform, volume, fold, detune and sync mode.

A special case: Filter, Filt Env and Cut LFO

The Filter Envelope and Cutoff LFO both modulate the Filter's cutoff — they share a target. Because of this, their Per-Osc / Linked modes are tied to the Filter's mode by a simple rule:

A modulator can have fewer or equal instances to its target — never more.

In practice:

  • If the Filter is in Linked mode (one shared filter), then the Filt Env and Cut LFO must also be in Linked mode. One envelope and one LFO drive the single shared filter.
  • If the Filter is in Per-Osc mode (four independent filters), then the Filt Env and Cut LFO can be in either mode. Set them to Linked and one envelope/LFO broadcasts to all four filters; set them to Per-Osc and each filter has its own dedicated envelope and LFO.

The same principle applies between the Filter and the Cut LFO directly — wherever one module modulates another, the modulator can have fewer instances than its target, but never more.

You'll see this play out as you switch modes: changing the Filter to Linked while the Filt Env or Cut LFO are in Per-Osc will adjust them to match, and trying to put a modulator into Per-Osc mode while its target is Linked will be blocked with a short dialog explaining what to switch first.

When to use which. Start in Linked when you're learning a new sound, or when all four oscillators are playing the same role (e.g. layered unison). Switch a specific module to Per-Osc the moment you want oscillators to behave differently from each other — for example, giving one oscillator its own filter envelope to make it pop out of the mix.

A Consistent Interface

Dante's UI is designed so that learning any one panel teaches you most of the others. A few things to notice as you explore:

  • Oscillator colours follow everywhere. Osc 1 is always blue, Osc 2 green, Osc 3 orange, Osc 4 magenta — in every panel, the Scope, the Preset Browser. Glance at any control and you instantly know which oscillator it belongs to.
  • Same layout, repeated. All four LFO panels share the same control structure (Rate / Range / Fade / Phase reset / Clock sync). Both envelopes share the same ADSR layout. Learn one, you can drive the others without re-reading anything.
  • Modes work the same way. The PER-OSC / LINKED toggle is in the same position on every panel that has it. The On/Off button is in the same position on every oscillator column.
  • Modulation is visible. The white indicator lines on parameter sliders show exactly which parameters are being moved by an LFO and by how much — so when something changes in the sound, you can always see why.

The payoff: even Dante's deepest features stay easy to navigate, because the colour-coding and consistent layout always tell you what you're looking at.

5The Oscillators

Each of Dante's four oscillators is fully independent — its own waveform, volume, fold, detune and pulse width.

5.1 Waveforms

Six waveforms are available per oscillator:

WaveformCharacter
SinePure, smooth tone. Great as a sub bass or for additive blending.
TriangleHollow, mellow. A softer alternative to sine with more upper harmonics.
SawBright, buzzy. The classic synth lead and bass waveform.
RampReverse saw. Same harmonic content as Saw but with different sync and phase behaviour.
PulseSquare / pulse wave. Pulse width is controlled by the PWM parameter.
NoiseWhite noise. Useful for percussive elements, hi-hats, wind / breath layers.

5.2 Per-Osc Controls

Underneath the waveform row, each oscillator has four parameter sliders:

SliderWhat it does
VolVolume of this oscillator in the master mix (0–100%).
FoldWavefolding amount. Higher values fold the waveform back on itself, adding harmonics and grit.
DetDetune in cents or semitones, depending on context. Lets you offset the pitch of this oscillator against the others.
PWMPulse Width Modulation amount. Only active when the waveform is Pulse — sets the duty cycle of the pulse wave.

Each oscillator also has an ON / OFF button at the top right of its column. Turning off an oscillator removes it from the mix entirely — it consumes no CPU when off.

5.3 Octave & Pitch

Between the waveform selector and the parameter sliders, each oscillator has an octave selector: -2 -1 0 +1 +2 +3. Tap to shift this oscillator's playing range up or down by full octaves relative to the played note.

Combine octave with the Det (detune) slider for fine pitch offsets between oscillators — this is the foundation of fat, layered sounds.

6Dualphonic Sync

Dualphonic Sync is what makes Dante different from every other iOS synth. It's worth understanding properly.

What's hard sync?

In classic synthesis, "hard sync" is when one oscillator (the master) forces another oscillator (the synced) to restart its waveform cycle every time the master completes a cycle. The synced oscillator's pitch becomes locked to the master's, but its harmonic content changes dramatically — producing characteristic glassy, aggressive, sweeping timbres.

What makes Dante's sync different?

Most synths offer one master/synced pair. Dante runs two independent hard-sync chains in parallel — chain A and chain B. Any oscillator can be assigned to either chain, or run completely independently.

Sync mode buttons

Underneath each oscillator's parameter row, you'll see two sync mode buttons. The choices are:

ModeMeaning
Master AThis oscillator drives sync chain A.
Synced AThis oscillator is synced to Master A.
Master BThis oscillator drives sync chain B.
Synced BThis oscillator is synced to Master B.
(neither)This oscillator runs independently, ignoring sync.

The DUALPHONIC SYNC button

In the master strip at the top of the page, the DUALPHONIC SYNC button cycles through every valid Dualphonic Sync configuration. One tap re-rolls the sync assignments across all four oscillators — the fastest way to hear what your current preset can sound like with different sync relationships.

Pair this with the dice button. Tap Generate to roll a fresh preset, then start tapping DUALPHONIC SYNC to walk through every sync arrangement of that sound. You'll discover textures you didn't know existed.

7Envelopes

Envelopes shape how a parameter changes over the duration of a note. Dante has two envelopes per oscillator: the Volume Envelope and the Filter Envelope.

7.1 Volume Envelope (Vol Env)

The Volume Envelope controls how loud the oscillator is from the moment a note is pressed until after it's released. Standard ADSR layout:

StageWhat it does
AttackHow quickly the volume ramps up after note-on. Fast = percussive; slow = pad-like.
DecayHow quickly the volume falls from the attack peak down to the sustain level.
SustainThe held volume level while the note is held down.
ReleaseHow quickly the volume fades to silence after note-off.

Vol Env supports linear or exponential curves, and includes a drone mode for sustained, key-tracking-free output.

Vol Env can be set per-oscillator (each oscillator has its own envelope) or linked (one envelope drives all four). Toggle this at the top of the Vol Env panel.

7.2 Filter Envelope (Filt Env)

The Filter Envelope shapes the filter cutoff over time, independent of the volume envelope. Same ADSR layout as the Volume Envelope.

The depth of the filter envelope's effect on the cutoff is set by the Env slider in the Filter panel — see 8. Filters.

Like Vol Env, Filt Env supports linear/exponential curves and can be per-osc or linked.

Mode constraint. Filt Env's Per-Osc / Linked mode is tied to the Filter's mode. You can have fewer or equal envelopes than filters, never more — see Per-Osc and Linked Modes for the full rule.

8Filters

Each oscillator has its own filter, with eight filter types available.

Filter types

TypeCharacter
LP 6Low-pass, 6dB/octave slope. Gentle, transparent darkening.
LP 12Low-pass, 12dB/octave. Classic synth low-pass, moderate squelch.
LP 18Low-pass, 18dB/octave. Deeper cut, more pronounced resonance.
LP 24Low-pass, 24dB/octave. Sharp, aggressive — the Moog-style ladder slope.
BPBand-pass. Removes lows and highs, leaving a tunable mid-band.
NotchNotch filter. Cuts a narrow frequency band, useful for phasing/sweep effects.
HPHigh-pass. Removes lows below the cutoff. Tightens bass-heavy sounds.
FmtFormant filter. Produces vowel-like resonances ("ah", "oh", etc.).

Filter parameters

SliderWhat it does
CutFilter cutoff frequency, in Hz (typically 20 Hz – 20 kHz, log-scaled).
ResResonance. Emphasises frequencies around the cutoff. At high settings, can self-oscillate.
EnvFilter envelope depth — how much the Filter Envelope opens or closes the filter from rest position.
KeyKey tracking. How much the cutoff follows the played note's pitch. 0% = static; 100% = fully tracks pitch.

Filters can be set per-oscillator or linked across all four.

The Filter is the target. Filt Env and Cut LFO both modulate the filter cutoff, so their Per-Osc / Linked modes are tied to the Filter's mode. If the Filter is Linked, those modulators must be Linked too — see Per-Osc and Linked Modes for the full rule.

9LFOs (Low-Frequency Oscillators)

LFOs are slow modulators that move a parameter up and down over time — think of them as automated knob-twiddling. Dante has four LFOs, each targeting a different parameter.

Shared LFO controls

Every LFO has the same set of base controls:

ControlWhat it does
WaveformShape of the LFO motion: sine, triangle, saw, ramp, square.
RateSpeed of the LFO, in Hz — or in note divisions when clock-synced.
RangeHow much the LFO moves the parameter (modulation depth).
FadeTime after note-on for the LFO to fade in from zero to full depth. Off disables fade-in (LFO starts at full).
Phase resetChoose Gate, 0°, 90°, 180° or 270° — controls where in its cycle the LFO restarts on each note-on.
Clock syncWhen enabled, locks the LFO rate to your host's tempo (or the standalone's BPM). Rate is then expressed in note divisions (1/4, 1/8, 1/16, etc.) instead of Hz.

All LFOs can be per-oscillator (each oscillator gets its own independent LFO) or linked (one LFO drives all four).

9.1 Cutoff LFO (Cut LFO)

Modulates the filter cutoff over time. The classic "wah-wah" sweeping effect.

Mode constraint. Cut LFO modulates the Filter, so its Per-Osc / Linked mode is tied to the Filter's mode. You can have fewer or equal LFOs than filters, never more — see Per-Osc and Linked Modes for the full rule.

9.2 Volume LFO (Vol LFO)

Modulates the oscillator volume — produces tremolo (amplitude modulation).

9.3 Wavefold LFO (Fold LFO)

Modulates the wavefolding amount. Produces evolving harmonic complexity — the fold parameter is one of Dante's strongest character controls.

9.4 Pitch LFO

Modulates the oscillator pitch — produces vibrato (frequency modulation).

Phase reset matters more than you'd think. Setting phase reset to Gate means the LFO restarts at the same point every time you play a note — useful for tight, predictable modulation. Setting it to 0°/90°/180°/270° picks a specific starting phase. Disabling reset (no specific value) lets the LFO free-run.

10Glide

Glide (portamento) makes the pitch slide smoothly from one note to the next instead of jumping. Essential for expressive leads and 303-style basslines.

Glide controls

ControlWhat it does
TimeHow long the slide takes between two pitches.
CurveShape of the slide — choose Exponential, Linear, Logarithmic or S-curve.
LegatoWhen enabled, glide only happens when notes overlap (true legato playing). Stacatto notes jump as normal.

Glide can be set per-oscillator or linked. Per-osc glide is one of Dante's most expressive features — for example, gliding only Osc 1 while Osc 2 jumps creates a "rubber-band" detuning effect.

13Random Preset Generator

Dante's random preset generator is musical, not chaotic. It produces usable, inspiring sounds — and lets you target exactly what gets randomised.

Opening the generator

Tap the dice button (🎲) anywhere it appears — in the master strip, in the Preset Browser header, or via a mapped MIDI CC (AUv3).

Base preset source

The top section chooses which category Dante draws inspiration from when rolling a new sound:

  • All / Favourites / Basic / Bass / Lead / Pad / Keys / FX / Drone / User — same categories as the Preset Browser.

Generate vs Tweak

  • Generate — rolls a completely new preset from scratch (within your chosen category and parameter selections).
  • Tweak — applies subtle, context-aware changes to the current preset. Use this when you like the direction but want variation.

Oscillator targeting

The Oscillators row lets you choose which oscillators the generator is allowed to randomise. Untick an oscillator to leave it untouched — useful when you have, say, a bass layer you want to keep stable while randomising the other three.

Parameter targeting

The Parameters grid lets you control exactly which kinds of changes are allowed:

  • Waveform, Volume, Octave, Detune, Pulse Width, Fold Amount, Osc On/Off, Vol Envelope, Filt Envelope, Filter, Effects, Volume LFO, Fold LFO, Cutoff LFO, Glide, Hard Sync.

Tick the ones you want to be randomised; untick anything you want left alone. Use the None link to clear all, then enable just the parameters you're curious about.

Example workflow. Like the filter and effects of your current preset but want a different rhythmic feel? Untick everything except Glide and Cutoff LFO, then tap Tweak a few times. You'll get fresh movement on the same sonic base.

14Scope

The Waveform Scope shows you exactly what each oscillator — and the master output — is producing in real time.

Tap SCOPE in the master strip to open the scope view. You'll see:

  • Master Output — the combined waveform of all four oscillators after filtering and effects.
  • Osc 1 / Osc 2 / Osc 3 / Osc 4 — each oscillator's current waveform, colour-matched to its column. The waveform label shows the current type (Sawtooth, Pulse, Sine, Triangle, etc.).

The scope is especially useful when you're combining oscillators with hard sync, wavefolding, or pitch LFO — you can see the harmonic interactions directly.

16App Preferences

App Preferences is where you control Dante's resource usage and check version information. It's organised into two sections: Performance Settings for managing CPU/DSP load, and App Information for version details and quick access to this user guide.

Opening App Preferences

Tap the cog icon (⚙️) in the top header, immediately to the right of the XY Pad button. The App Preferences dialog opens; like the Scope, Preset Browser and Random Generator, it can be resized to fit the space available on your screen.

16.1 Performance Settings

Dante runs comfortably on every supported device with the default settings. Performance Settings exist mainly for AUv3 host situations — when you have many plugins loaded in your host and need to manage the total CPU/DSP load across the session.

Reverb Quality

The audible quality of the reverb depends on how many parallel delay lines it runs internally. More lines = denser, smoother reverb tail. Fewer lines = lighter CPU load.

SettingInternal linesCharacter
Off0 (bypassed)No reverb. Maximum CPU savings.
Light2Lo-fi, grainy reverb. Very low CPU.
Standard4Balanced reverb quality. Moderate CPU.
Full8Highest-quality reverb. Default.

The default is Full. Reach for the lower tiers only when you're trying to claw back CPU in a busy AUv3 session, or if you specifically want the character of a sparser reverb.

Force Linked Effects

By default, each oscillator can run its own independent reverb and delay. While powerful, this multiplies the effects' CPU cost by up to four.

Turning Force Linked Effects on locks the Effects panel into linked mode: one reverb and one delay shared across all four oscillators. This single change can reduce DSP load by more than half in effect-heavy patches.

If you load (or are already running) a preset with per-oscillator effects when you enable Force Linked Effects, Dante silently collapses the effects to linked mode, taking Osc 1's effects settings as the shared source. The sound of the other oscillators' effects is replaced; their settings are not preserved.

While Force Linked Effects is on, if you visit the Effects tab and try to switch back to per-oscillator mode, Dante will display a short dialog explaining that the change is being blocked by the Force Linked Effects setting, and pointing you to App Preferences where you can turn it off.

When to use these settings

Dante's defaults are designed to perform well across all supported devices. You typically only need to touch Performance Settings when:

  • You're running Dante alongside many other AUv3 plugins inside a host, and the host's total DSP load is getting too high.
  • You're stacking multiple instances of Dante in the same session.
  • You want the deliberate character of a sparser reverb (Light or Standard) rather than the lush default.

In all of these cases, enabling Force Linked Effects is usually the biggest single win. Lowering Reverb Quality recovers additional headroom on top of that.

App Preferences persist between sessions, not per preset. Your choices apply to every preset you load. Sharing or saving a preset never overrides another user's preferences.

18MIDI (Full mapping AUv3)

Dante supports MIDI note input on all platforms. The AUv3 version adds extensive MIDI CC mapping for parameters and UI controls.

Note input

Standard MIDI note on/off triggers Dante. Note velocity, pitch bend, mod wheel and aftertouch are all received.

CC mapping (AUv3)

The AUv3 version provides MIDI CC mapping across virtually every parameter — filter cutoff, fold amount, LFO rates, envelope stages, glide time, effect mix, and more.

Button MIDI mapping (AUv3)

In addition to parameter mapping, the following buttons can be driven directly from MIDI:

  • Generate (random preset)
  • Tweak
  • Dualphonic Sync (cycle valid sync combinations)

UI window MIDI mapping (AUv3)

You can also map MIDI to open/close UI windows — useful when you're in a live performance or DAW workflow and don't want to touch the screen:

  • Scope
  • Preset Browser
  • XY Pad
  • Random Generator

  3. Paste the preset into the form's preset field.
  4. Submit.

The whole flow requires you to deliberately copy, navigate, paste and submit — Dante never sends anything automatically. See our Privacy Policy for the full disclosure on what happens with submitted presets.

20Tips & Workflow

Start from a preset, don't start from blank

Building a sound from scratch is slow. Roll the dice, find something you like, then modify. Even pros usually start from an existing patch.

Use Tweak more than Generate

Generate gives you a brand-new sound — useful for inspiration but easy to lose your direction. Once you have a promising starting point, switch to Tweak. You'll iterate faster and end up with sounds that feel intentional.

Cycle DUALPHONIC SYNC to find unique textures

Most people leave sync alone. Don't. After you've found a sound, tap the DUALPHONIC SYNC button 5–10 times — you'll often discover a variation more interesting than the original.

Pay attention to the white LFO indicators

When you can't tell what's making a parameter move, look for the moving white line inside the slider. It's showing you exactly what an LFO is doing in real time — invaluable when sounds get complex.

Use linked mode when you're learning

Per-oscillator everything gets overwhelming fast. When you're learning, start with most modules in linked mode (one envelope drives all four oscillators) and only break individual modules out to per-osc when you need that specific control.

Keep the Scope open

The Scope teaches you what each waveform actually sounds like by showing it visually. After a few sessions you'll start predicting what changes will do before you make them — that's when sound design starts to feel intuitive.

21Troubleshooting

I can't hear anything

  • Check the master Output meter at the top of the screen — is it moving when you play?
  • Check the Gain slider isn't at zero.
  • Make sure at least one oscillator is ON and its Vol slider is above zero.
  • If using AUv3, check that the host's track is unmuted and routed correctly.

The DSP percentage is high

Dante runs comfortably on every supported device, so high DSP usually means your AUv3 host has a lot loaded into it. In order of impact:

  • Open App Preferences (cog icon) and enable Force Linked Effects in the Performance Settings section. One shared reverb and delay across all four oscillators can cut effects CPU by more than half.
  • In the same panel, drop Reverb Quality a tier — Full → Standard, or Standard → Light. The difference is most noticeable on long reverb tails.
  • Turn OFF any oscillators you're not using. An off oscillator consumes no CPU.
  • Heavy modulation (multiple LFOs at fast rates, all running per-oscillator) adds up. Where it doesn't change the sound, run LFOs in linked mode.
  • If your host gives you control, raising the audio buffer size also gives more headroom (at the cost of slightly more latency).
